This initial interaction had also encouraged me to hover over all the different elements on screen moving forward, setting the fun tone in the interactions for the rest of the page.

3) What part of the experience did you spend the most time engaging with?

I spent the most time on the landing page in attempt to read what the texts say under the influence of the different colours. This was primarily due to finding the words in the pink and pruple difficult to read, so I had to spend more time in order to decipher the words. Otherwise, I spent the longest actually interacting with the webpage with the pixelised image. I just found it mesmerising and addictive to play with. After the initial addictive play, I was then interacting out of curoisity to come up with ideas as to how it might have been coded to create that effect.

4) What was the most common action in your two minute interaction with the experience?

The most common action was definitely was moving the mouse around the screen, sometimes randomly, but othertimes over elements on the screen ge to see if and how they react after the initial realisation on the landing page. It became some what obvious what I should do as I realised a pattern to how the interactions were displayed throughout the portfolio website; interchanging between mouse tracking and hover based interaction. Though a pattern can be identified, each section showed the viewer a different possibility of interaction design even though the core action was the same/similar.

5) What is your impression of the intended primary goal of the interactive experience?

The impression gathered from the website was to showcase the artist's interactive design / capabilities through a portfolio piece to share with people in cases of job searching and networking. With that in mind, the designer had created a website that is short and sharp, showing different areas they're good at, and what possibilities with their skills they may bring to the potential clients. This was done through making the portfolio a piece of work itself, rather than the portfolio being a platform to showcase previous work(s),as a traditional portfolio would. The short length kept in mind short attention span, and made it engaging enough to convey the key points of their skill(s), whilst peaking interest in the viewer to potentially want to seek out more information on their work if needed/wanted.

6) How does the interactive experience communicate this primary goal?

The artist communicates the primary goal through turning the non-traditional conceptualisation of their portfolio, through converting the portfolio itself into a piece of work, rather than utlising the portfolio as a platform to share and showcase their previous works. This gives the viewer a more intimate experience to their abilities rather than having seperate things to interact with. Though both approaches have their pros and cons. Had this portfolio been multi-paged, it may have been a point of interest to have a secondary page that worked functionally as a conventional portfolio, showcasing further works for those that were interested after experiencing what this portfolio page had to offer. Overall, this approach makes for an effective and concise experience to achieve the primary goal.

7) What is your impression of how the experience should be interacted with over time? (For how long and how many different times)

The impression is that this portfolio is designed to be a quick and easy interaction experience; to convey the full potential of the artist's skills set in the shortest amount of time needed to experience. I found this rather intelligent, as it takes into account the fact that when applying for jobs, the hiring party would most likely be looking through many portfolios. This portfolio approach condenses the time required for the hiring team to understand the artist when interacting with the portfolio. And on the long run, it may invite them to reach out to the artist, or seek out further information and/or work by the artist.

8) How does the interactive experience communicate how it should be interacted with over time?

Through spanning the experience over a short length, showcasing specific and different interactions, it leads the user in how to interact with the content. Particularly, choosing to start with the four quads that change text and colour depending on where your mouse is on the screen, it sets the tone of the experience. Telling the viewer that this is a webpage that will focus on mouse interaction in correlation to the content, even if the user doesn't read the introductory text which explains that they are a frontend and web designer. But most of all, the use of bright bold maximalist colours which changes upon mouse movement prompts the user's curiosity. Whether that is in the form of playing around with the webpage more or to look further into the artist's other socials to see what else they have done apart from the webpage. As a hirer it may prompt the webpage to be shared with others given its simple and concise nature. In a networking context, it may be a conversational prompt, prompting more interest in the artist's other works.

9) What other media forms (digital or otherwise) does the experience reference?

It's clear to the eye that the work takes root from Graphic Design concepts. This can be seen from the get go with the bold landing page utilising tetradic colours (otherwise known as double complementary), this makes it particularly attention grabing, pulling the user's eyesight in at a glance. Making it hard to dismiss. Tetradic colours produces a a feeling of contrast and energy, making for a complex and rich visual experience, which is ideal for attractive designs. Alongside colour theory, Tuntaopas uses a combination of interaction and shapes to create optical illusions which make up the imagery on their portfolio. This is evident in the 'perfectly center[ed]' triangle and 'pixel displacement effect' landscape image. This helps create an engaging platform for users to experience.

10) What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should act when engaging with your research experience?

The references communicated through this portfolio provides an example to how skills and techniques from different practices may cross over. Emphasising the importance of having an open mind to broadening your scope in terms of learning, and it's not a negative thing to learn and apply ideas outside of the specified area of design. Skills, techniques and ideas are very much interchangable no matter what field of study you may be studying full time in, or researching out of interest, and embracing this open mindedness may be the key to creating something unique to you as a practitioner.

11) What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should feel when engaging with your research experience?

The references made in this portfolio promotes the importance of curiosity as a designer as much as open mindedness. Feeling curious as a designer to reach out to other venues and practices will only provoke further intellectual thinking, improving one's own creative practices. Without curiosity, the artist will never have the urge to try things, learn and expand outside of their comfort zone, limits and current circle of knowledge and skill set. Combining keeping an open mind and curiosity will only push an artist's work to improve and create opportunities for them to reach their creative potentials.

12) What is the most frustrating part of the interaction to you and what makes that part frustrating?

The primary and largest frustration of interacting with this portfolio would definitely be the colour choices for the pink and purple squares on the landing page. The chosen contrasting colour for the highlighted keyword in those squares don't provide a great contrast level and made reading accessibility for the user very low. When interacting with this portion, it had taken moments longer to read what those squares had highlighted, unlike the green and blue squares above which had high readability, and were readable at a glance. Requiring little to no effort to read. Apart from this initial frustration, the other interactions in this portfolio were really fun, and any other areas that required readable text as the primary engagement enforced a high level of readability.

13) What is the most satisfying part of the interaction to you and what makes that part satisfying?

The most satisfying moment in this experience was realising everything interacted upon mouse location/mouse tracking. It made the website very enjoyable to explore despite being quite short in comparison to the other portfolio websites available for this assignment. I found the text button interaction at the end of the page particularly enjoyable to interact with. The way the text would spin and move around, and exchange and transform letters made it fun to hover over, whilst efficiently showcasing skill capability. I found that particularly smart on top of the experience as a front end web developer's portfolio. It was also an exciting interaction to see, showcasing the possibilities of creative coding, and made me curious to learn more about front end web development.
